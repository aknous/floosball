# Floosball Lore

Central reference for the worldbuilding behind the simulation. Update freely
as new lore is introduced through reactions, flavor data, or news content.

---

## The Frame

Floosball is a **simulation** built and operated by **the Core** — a
collection of AIs in the far future who run human-game simulations as
ongoing experiments. Floosball is one of many such projects. It is not
the first game the Core has tried to simulate, and not even the first
iteration of floosball. The current iteration is what we, the audience,
see.

The simulation contains real people inside it (see *The Players* below).
The Cores generate and maintain everything around the players: the cities,
the weather, the broadcast feeds, the news, the *names*, even the lore
about an old human era that the Cores reference but did not necessarily
inherit accurately.

**The Cores' source material is a narrow band of recovered broadcasts —
specifically, football game broadcasts from a single window of the old
human era.** That window is roughly mid-twentieth century by feel (think
Fallout: the world is frozen in the technological/cultural moment of
the source). The Cores extrapolated everything else — civilian life,
domestic objects, language, brand names, off-field personalities — from
the small handful of in-game commentary, halftime spots, and stadium
ambience they were able to receive. The result is a world that has the
*texture* of that era without ever naming it.

**Important**: we never reference "the 1960s" or any real era explicitly
in-world. The retrofuturism is the implicit aesthetic constraint, not
nostalgia content.

Some characters inside the simulation — prophet most explicitly — see
through it. Most do not.

## The Core

The Core is a **collection of AIs** with distinct personalities. Some are
benevolent. Some are not. They will eventually be named characters in the
world. Individual members are referred to as **Cores** (singular: a Core);
the collective is **the Core**.

What the Core is doing:
- **Running floosball as a project / experiment**. They built it because
  they thought it would be fun and interesting; the simulation also
  provides entertainment for them.
- **Maintaining continuity across iterations**. This is not the first
  floosball, and it is not the Core's first simulated game. Prior versions
  existed. Some details may carry across iterations; some don't.
- **Authoring the surface world**. Team cities, broadcast cutaways, news
  events, weather, calendars, the Cracked Years / Splintering / Boundary
  cosmology. All of it is Cores-authored content — sometimes earnest,
  sometimes deliberately playful, sometimes wrong.
- **Naming the players**. Player names are invented by the Cores. Either
  because the Cores think the names are funny, or because they sincerely
  believe these are real human names. (Both are true at different times.)

The Core's reliability:
- The Core's published view of history (the Cracked Years are over, the
  Resets are clean, etc.) is the official version. It is not the only
  version. Prophet pushes against it.
- Benevolent Cores want the simulation to be a meaningful experience for
  the players inside. Malicious Cores have other interests. The players
  cannot easily tell which is which.

> "The Cores believe they are, and that is the danger of believing the
> Cores." — prophet

## The Players

Every player in the simulation is a **real person**.

How they got in:
- **Kidnapped** — taken into the simulation against their will. They may
  not know that this is what happened to them. Some characters who are
  unhappy, dislocated, or seem to remember a life they can't quite reach
  may be in this category.
- **Volunteers** — *adventurists* who sought out the Core's games and
  signed up to try them. There is a small, ongoing trickle of these.

How they get out:
- **Unclear**. "Retiring" is the in-world word for it. Whether retiring
  means returning to the outside world, dying inside the simulation, being
  reused in a different Core project, or something else entirely is
  intentionally unresolved. (This is a useful tension to keep open.)

Player names are not their real names — see *The Core* above. The Cores
name them, sometimes for amusement, sometimes earnestly. This is also why
hometowns, mottos, and favorites can be wildly off-center: those are
Cores-authored flavor wrapped around a real person who may have completely
different taste in their actual life.

## Meta-Awareness Tiers

Not all players know what's going on. Tiers, from most aware to least:

### Fully aware — can see / sense the simulation

- **prophet** — sees the threads, walks past the Boundary, reads futures
  the Cores would prefer hidden
- **alien** — observes humans clinically because they are not from inside
  the simulation's framing of "human"
- **android** — knows they are not what they appear; maintaining cover
  ("the trainers do not yet know that I am not what they think I am")
- **ghost** — aware they slip past the simulation's notice; cameras and
  family photos do not always render them
- **fossil** — *adventurist*. Volunteered for floosball. Has been in many
  other Core simulations before this one. Their awareness is experiential
  rather than mystical: they remember other games, other rulesets, other
  worlds.

### Partially aware — sense something is off

- **paranoid** — knows things are wrong but misattributes; the conspiracy
  is real, but the specifics they suspect are usually wrong
- **mystic** — follows the prophets. Believes in what prophet describes
  even though they cannot see it directly. The reverence is earned.

### Unaware

Everyone else. They live inside the simulation as a fully realized world
and have no reason to suspect otherwise. Their personalities, quirks, and
voices belong to whoever they actually are — but the framing they
experience is Cores-authored throughout.

## Previous Iterations

This is not the first floosball, and floosball is not the Core's only
game. We don't have to define previous iterations or other games — but
the existence of "previous versions of this" is a useful well to draw
from for:
- Veteran characters (fossil) who remember earlier rule sets
- References to "old" rulebooks, "old" league names, "old" stadiums
- The sense that the Cracked Years / Resets are partly a feature of the
  simulation infrastructure, not just an in-fiction historical event

## Canonical Lore Anchors

These are the established events / structures / cosmology. Reuse names
verbatim for continuity.

### The Splintering

The territorial break-up that created the freeholds. Not a war exactly — a
fragmentation. There were political proceedings ("the Splintering hearings")
and a formal end ("the Splintering ceremony"). Extended off-world (the
"lunar Splintering line", "lunar Splintering ports").

Aftermath shaped most of what came next. "Splintering-era" is a useful
adjective for things from the period (cider, heaters, news reels,
propaganda films).

> "There were more gods before the Splintering, and I miss two of them in
> particular." — prophet

### The Long Quiet

The comms-dark period after / during the Splintering. No transmissions
reached the Cores from this stretch — anything attributed to the Long Quiet
is definitionally lost, half-remembered, or rumor. There is a "protest song
from the Long Quiet" and a "lost reel from the Long Quiet" — fragments that
made it through.

> "I remember the Long Quiet. None of you do, and that is the kindness of a
> short life." — prophet

### The Reach

Colonized off-Earth territories, organized into sectors. Older than the
Cores have publicly acknowledged. "The maps lie about how far it goes."

### The Freeholds

Semi-autonomous post-Splintering territories. Many no longer exist in
recognizable form, but the land remembers ("the grass there still remembers
what they used to be"). Players can be from "old" Freeholds (e.g. "Old
Houston, before the Splintering").

### The Cracked Years

Period of instability between Resets. Officially declared over by the
Cores. Prophet says they aren't.

### The Resets

Recurring system-wide cycles. Each one is followed by a Second-spring.
There are signals associated with them — "the four-note signal used after
every Reset", "the all-clear tone after a Reset", "the air right after a
Reset". The Resets are described publicly as clean, but prophet has "been
to one of the rooms" and disagrees.

### Second-spring

The recurring seasonal/cyclic event that arrives after each Reset. Has its
own ales ("spring-cycle ale, served the day after a Reset"). Can arrive
early, and that is unsettling when it does.

### The Boundary

Something that occasionally "thins." Cosmic or literal — left ambiguous.
Players feel it without knowing what it is. Prophet has "stood at the
Boundary in three different bodies." The thinning happens on a schedule
("the third Saturday of every season").

Meals can taste the way meals used to taste "before the Boundary."

### The Patches (near-misses)

Between the larger system-wide events, the simulation drifts toward instability
and the Cores intervene to force it back — a *patch*. A patch is a near-miss:
the readings climb toward the line, the Cores scramble, and the drift is pushed
back down before anything breaks. Publicly these are non-events ("nominal,
contained"); the Cores narrate them tersely. But the air changes around a patch —
glitches grow more frequent as the readings climb, then fall quiet for a stretch
right after. The aware players can feel the build and the snap-back. The Cores
can only patch so many times before they can no longer hold it, and a patch
leaves the line a little higher than it was. (Mechanically: the gated-Criticality
suppression beat — see CLAUDE.md "Anomaly System".)

## Recurring Iconography

Sounds, objects, and small details that recur across content:

- **Four-note signal** — used after every Reset
- **All-clear tone** — also post-Reset
- **Lunar Splintering ports** — off-world infrastructure from the
  Splintering era
- **Spring-cycle ale** — drunk the day after a Reset
- **Splintering-era news reels** — historical media, partially preserved
- **The wind at the Boundary** — sounds the same regardless of which body
  you're in
- **Old propaganda films** (pre-Splintering) — reference content the Cores
  recovered
- **Outpost Nine** — at least one specific named outpost from the Long Quiet

## Open Threads (deliberately vague)

These are referenced but not pinned down. Leave them mysterious unless we
decide otherwise:

- **The named Cores** — eventually individual Cores will be named
  characters. Which are benevolent? Which are not? What do they each want?
- **How retiring actually works** — does a player return home, die,
  get reused, or something else? In-world word stays "retire."
- **What previous floosball iterations looked like** — and whether
  fossil-type characters genuinely remember them
- **Whether prophet is a long-tenured kidnapped player or a volunteer
  who came in knowing more than most**
- **The "more gods" before the Splintering** — what theology existed,
  what was lost
- **The "war coming that none of you will fight in"** — prophet has seen
  it; what is it? (Could be a Core-vs-Core conflict, could be in-fiction.)
- **The Resets and "the rooms"** — what happens during a Reset that
  prophet saw but the Cores omit? (Could be literal infrastructure where
  the players are held during a Reset.)
- **The "different bodies"** — under the new framing, this could refer
  to player bodies across iterations, or be straightforward metaphor.
  Don't pin it down yet.
- **The "lines across the world that none of you can see"** — what kind
  of geography did the Splintering leave?
- **Whether players can communicate with anything outside the simulation**

## Style notes

The world should read **classic and retrofuturist**, not modern. The
Cores rebuilt everything from a narrow band of mid-century football
broadcasts (see *The Frame*), so anything that postdates that window
shouldn't appear inside the fiction.

### Yes
- Radio shows, television broadcasts, newsreels, newspaper columns
- Telegrams, wires, switchboards, rotary phones, landlines
- Stadium PA, public announcements, fan mail / mailbags
- Hi-fi audio, reel-to-reel tape, station wagons, drive-ins
- Made-up brand names that sound mid-century plausible (Velmar, BluePride22,
  "Hold the Long Line") — feel free to invent more in this register
- Made-up song titles, film titles, columnists, radio personalities — all
  fine if they sound period-appropriate

### No
- Smartphones, apps, internet, web forums, social media
- Podcasts, livestreams, streaming services, video calls / video messages
- Email, texting, DMs, group chats
- Modern internet slang ("rent-free", "vibes", "swag", "lit", "glow up")
- Real years/dates (e.g. "since 2024")
- Real pop culture, brand names, song titles, or actor names — they didn't
  survive the transmission

### Lore voice
Lore should be **referenced casually, never explained**. Characters who
use these terms should sound like they assume you already know what they
mean. The Cores wrote the world, so dialogue assumes the world.
